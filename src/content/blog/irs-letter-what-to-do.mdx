The IRS sends millions of letters every year. Most are routine — adjustments, requests for information, identity-verification prompts, or simple math corrections. A small share are serious. The trick is knowing which is which without panicking.

## Step 1: Read the notice number

Every IRS letter has a notice number in the upper right corner, like CP14, CP2000, or LT11. That number tells you what kind of notice it is:

- **CP14** — You have a balance due. Usually because the IRS adjusted your return or you under-paid.
- **CP2000** — A mismatch between what you reported and what the IRS has on file. This is a proposal — you do not necessarily owe what they say.
- **LT11** — Notice of intent to levy. This is serious and time-sensitive.
- **5071C** — Identity verification request. Usually a good thing; it means they caught a potentially fraudulent filing.

## Step 2: Check the response deadline

Every notice has a response date. Even if the notice is correct, ignoring it is the single worst thing you can do — interest and penalties accrue, and a CP2000 proposal becomes assessed tax 30 days after the response window closes.

## Step 3: Compare against your records

Pull the return year referenced in the notice. Compare the numbers the IRS lists against what you filed. Mismatches happen for innocent reasons — a corrected 1099 the IRS got but you did not, a duplicate W-2 reported by an employer, or a payment from a side gig you reported on a Schedule C that the IRS thinks is unreported income.

## Step 4: Decide whether to respond yourself

For very small balance-due notices where the math is right, paying online ends the matter. For anything else — proposed adjustments, identity verification, audit letters, or levy notices — get help before you respond. The response you send becomes part of your case file.

## Step 5: Save the envelope

The postmark date sometimes matters for deadlines. Keep the envelope until the issue is resolved.
