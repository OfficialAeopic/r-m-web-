Whenever someone forms a new business in Texas, this question comes up: should it be an LLC or an S-Corp?

The short answer is that LLC and S-Corp are not the same kind of thing. An **LLC** is a legal entity formed under Texas law. An **S-Corp** is a tax election you make with the IRS. You can have an LLC that is taxed as an S-Corp, an LLC taxed as a sole proprietorship, or an LLC taxed as a partnership. The structures are layered, not exclusive.

## What an LLC actually does for you

The LLC handles **liability**. It is a legal wrapper that separates business debts and obligations from your personal assets. Formation is straightforward in Texas (a Certificate of Formation with the Secretary of State and an Operating Agreement), and the annual maintenance is light (Franchise Tax filings, but no income tax at the entity level by default).

## What an S-Corp election does for you

The S-Corp election handles **how the IRS taxes your business income**. Without it, a single-member LLC is taxed as a sole proprietorship — all profit flows through to your personal return and is subject to self-employment tax (15.3% on the first ~$168,600 of net earnings, plus 2.9% Medicare on the rest).

With an S-Corp election, you become an employee of your own company. You pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (subject to payroll taxes), and remaining profits are taken as distributions that are *not* subject to self-employment tax. For profitable businesses, this can reduce overall tax. For low-profit businesses, the cost of payroll administration and the requirement to take a reasonable salary can wipe out any savings.

## When the S-Corp election usually makes sense

There is no bright line, but a general guideline: when your net business income consistently exceeds **$60,000–$80,000** per year, the S-Corp election starts to pay for itself. Below that, payroll administration costs (whether DIY or outsourced) often exceed the SE tax savings.

## What to consider before electing

- **Reasonable salary requirement.** The IRS expects you to pay yourself fair-market salary for the work you do. Pay yourself too little and you risk reclassification of distributions back to wages.
- **Payroll filings.** You will file Forms 941 (quarterly), 940 (annual), W-2 (annual), plus state filings. This is a real ongoing cost.
- **Single class of stock rule.** If you have business partners, S-Corp ownership rules are more restrictive than LLC rules.
- **State considerations.** Texas does not have a state income tax, but the Franchise Tax applies to most entities. S-Corp election is federal.
